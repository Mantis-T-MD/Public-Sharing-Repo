Ch 1 — Fundamentals

Newton's Laws

  1. A body at rest stays at rest (or constant velocity) unless acted on by a net external force.
  2. F = ma — Force equals mass × acceleration.
  3. Every action has an equal and opposite reaction.

Units

SI: Force = Newton (N) = kg·m/s² 1 kN = 1000 N US: Force = pound (lb) 1 kip = 1000 lb Weight: W = mg (g = 9.81 m/s² or 32.2 ft/s²)
Prof's Key Conversions: 1 Short Ton (American) = 2000 lbs 1 Tonne (SI/Metric) = 1000 kg Mass: lbf = slug × 32.2 ft/s² 1 ft = 12 in 1 m = 100 cm = 1000 mm SI mass = kg → W = kg × 9.81 = N US mass = slug → W = slug × 32.2 = lbf

Vectors vs Scalars

  • Scalar: magnitude only (mass, temperature, energy)
  • Vector: magnitude + direction (force, velocity, moment)
  • Vectors add by parallelogram law or triangle rule, NOT algebraically
Ch 2 — Statics of Particles (Force Vectors)

2.1–2.3: 2D Force Systems

Rectangular Components:

Fx = F cos θ Fy = F sin θ F = √(Fx² + Fy²) θ = tan⁻¹(Fy / Fx)

Resultant of Concurrent Forces:

Rx = ΣFx = F₁x + F₂x + F₃x + ... Ry = ΣFy = F₁y + F₂y + F₃y + ... R = √(Rx² + Ry²) θR = tan⁻¹(Ry / Rx)

Particle Equilibrium (2D):

ΣFx = 0 ΣFy = 0

Two equations → can solve for two unknowns.

Strategy: Orient axes to align with as many forces as possible — reduces components to calculate.

2.4–2.5: 3D Force Systems

Cartesian Vector Notation:

F = Fxî + Fyĵ + Fzk̂

Direction Cosines:

cos α = Fx/F cos β = Fy/F cos γ = Fz/F cos²α + cos²β + cos²γ = 1 (ALWAYS!)

Unit Vector Along a Line (A→B):

r_AB = (xB − xA)î + (yB − yA)ĵ + (zB − zA)k̂ λ_AB = r_AB / |r_AB| F = F · λ_AB (force vector along AB)

Dot Product:

A · B = AxBx + AyBy + AzBz = |A||B| cos θ θ = cos⁻¹(A · B / |A||B|) (angle between vectors)

Particle Equilibrium (3D):

ΣFx = 0 ΣFy = 0 ΣFz = 0

Three equations → three unknowns.

Common mistake: Forgetting cos²α + cos²β + cos²γ = 1. Use this to check or find a missing direction cosine.
Ch 3 — Rigid Bodies: Moments & Couples ★ High Emphasis

3.1: Moment of a Force About a Point

Definition: The tendency of a force to cause rotation about a point.

M_O = r × F

Scalar form (2D):

|M_O| = F · d (d = perpendicular distance from O to line of action)

Vector cross product (3D):

M_O = r × F = |î ĵ k̂ | |rx ry rz| |Fx Fy Fz| M_O = (ryFz − rzFy)î − (rxFz − rzFx)ĵ + (rxFy − ryFx)k̂

Varignon's Theorem:

M_O = r × (F₁ + F₂) = r × F₁ + r × F₂

The moment of a force = sum of moments of its components. Decompose F into components, take moments of each — often simpler.

Key insight: For moment calculation, r can be ANY vector from O to ANY point on the line of action of F. Choose the simplest one.

Sign convention (2D):

+ CCW (counterclockwise)
− CW (clockwise)

⚠ Two Methods for Moment (Prof Tests Both!)

Method 1 — Scalar (Perpendicular Distance):

M_O = ± F · d (d = perpendicular distance to line of action)

Quick when d is obvious. Determine CW/CCW by inspection.

Method 2 — Vector Product (Components):

M_O = r × F = (x·Fy − y·Fx) k̂ (for 2D, with r = xî + yĵ)

Always works. r = position vector from O to ANY point on F's line of action.

Method 3 — Varignon / Component Method (Fx, Fy separately):

M_O = Fx·(⊥ dist to Fx) ± Fy·(⊥ dist to Fy)

Decompose F into Fx and Fy at the application point, then sum each component's moment separately. Often the easiest method on exams.

Prof's class quiz format: Solve the same moment problem using BOTH the vector product (r × F) AND the Fx/Fy component method. Show all work for both. Expect this on the exam.

3.2: Moment About an Axis

Project moment onto axis direction:

M_L = λ_L · (r × F)

Scalar triple product (determinant form):

M_L = |λx λy λz| |rx ry rz| |Fx Fy Fz|

Where λ_L is the unit vector along the axis, r is position vector from any point on the axis to any point on the force's line of action.

Key rule: If F is parallel to axis L, or if F intersects axis L, then M_L = 0.

3.4: Couples

Definition: Two forces, equal magnitude, opposite direction, separated by distance d.

M = r × F |M| = F · d

Properties (CRITICAL):

  • A couple produces ROTATION ONLY (no translation) — net force = 0
  • Moment of a couple is the SAME about ANY point (free vector)
  • A couple can be moved anywhere without changing its effect
  • Couples can be added as vectors: M_R = M₁ + M₂ + ...
  • A couple can be replaced by any other couple with the same moment vector
Strategy for couple problems: Since the moment is the same about any point, choose the most convenient point to compute it. You don't need to pick a specific reference point.

Simplifying Force Systems (3.1 + 3.4 Combined)

Any force system can be reduced to:

  1. A single resultant force R = ΣF (acting at a chosen point O)
  2. A resultant couple moment M_O = ΣM_O (about that point O)
R = ΣF M_O = Σ(r × F) + ΣM_couples

Special cases:

  • If R = 0 → system is a couple (pure rotation)
  • If M_O = 0 → system reduces to a single force through O
  • If R ≠ 0 and M_O ⊥ R → can reduce to a single force at a new location

Force-Couple Reduction Procedure (Exam Favorite)

Given: Multiple forces F₁, F₂, ... and couples M₁, M₂, ... → Reduce to equivalent system at point O.

1Move each force to O — Each force moved to O introduces a couple: M_i = r_i × F_i
2Sum all forces at O: R = ΣF_i
3Sum all couples: M_O = Σ(r_i × F_i) + ΣM_couples
4Result: One force R at O + one couple M_O
Key insight: Moving a force to a different point ALWAYS adds a couple. The couple equals the moment of the original force about the new point. This is reversible: force-couple at O can become a single force at a new location if R ≠ 0.
Ch 4 — Equilibrium of Rigid Bodies ★ High Emphasis

4.1: Conditions for Equilibrium

2D Equilibrium (3 equations, 3 unknowns):

ΣFx = 0 ΣFy = 0 ΣM_A = 0

Alternative equation sets (all valid):

Set 1: ΣFx = 0, ΣFy = 0, ΣM_A = 0 (standard) Set 2: ΣFx = 0, ΣM_A = 0, ΣM_B = 0 (A-B not ⊥ to x-axis) Set 3: ΣM_A = 0, ΣM_B = 0, ΣM_C = 0 (A,B,C not collinear)
Strategy: Take moments about a point where unknown forces intersect — eliminates those unknowns from the equation, solving directly for a third.

3D Equilibrium (6 equations, 6 unknowns):

ΣFx = 0 ΣFy = 0 ΣFz = 0 ΣMx = 0 ΣMy = 0 ΣMz = 0

4.2: Support Reactions

Support TypeReactions# Unknowns
Roller / Rocker 1 force (⊥ to surface) 1
Pin / Hinge 2 forces (Ax, Ay) 2
Fixed / Built-in 2 forces + 1 moment (Ax, Ay, MA) 3
Link / Cable 1 force (along link direction) 1
Smooth surface 1 force (⊥ to surface, push only) 1
Ball-and-socket (3D) 3 forces (Ax, Ay, Az) 3
Fixed (3D) 3 forces + 3 moments 6
Cable rule: Cables/ropes can only PULL (tension). They cannot push. Always assume tension and if you get a negative value, the cable is slack (zero force, not compression).

Determinacy & Stability

Statically determinate (2D): # unknowns = 3 (can solve with 3 equilibrium equations)

Statically indeterminate: # unknowns > 3 (need additional equations — deformation, etc.)

Stability checks:

  • Improper constraints: All reactions concurrent (meet at a point) or parallel → unstable
  • 3 unknowns with 3 equations doesn't guarantee stability — check geometric arrangement
  • If all supports provide only horizontal reactions → no vertical equilibrium → unstable
Ch 7 — Internal Forces (Beams)

7.1: Internal Forces in Members

Method of Sections: Cut the member at the point of interest. Internal forces on the cut face:

  • N = Normal force (along member axis)
  • V = Shear force (perpendicular to member axis)
  • M = Bending moment

Procedure:

1Find external reactions (full FBD first)
2Cut at desired section
3Draw FBD of one side (choose simpler side)
4Show N, V, M at cut face with positive sign convention
5Apply ΣFx = 0, ΣFy = 0, ΣM_cut = 0

7.2: Beams — Shear & Moment

Sign Convention (standard beam):

+V: Causes CW rotation of segment
−V: Causes CCW rotation of segment
+M: Causes beam to "smile" (concave up)
−M: Causes beam to "frown" (concave down)

On a left-side FBD:

+V = upward on left face (↑) +N = outward (tension) +M = counterclockwise on left face

Key relationships:

dV/dx = −w(x) (w = distributed load intensity, + downward) dM/dx = V

These mean:

  • Where w = 0: V is constant, M is linear
  • Where w = constant: V is linear, M is parabolic
  • At a concentrated force: V has a jump discontinuity
  • At a concentrated moment: M has a jump discontinuity
  • V = 0 at a point → M is at a local max or min there

SFD/BMD Construction (Prof's 7-Example Method)

1Reactions first — Draw full FBD, solve for all support reactions.
2Start from left — Begin V at x = 0. At left support, V jumps by the reaction force.
3Walk right — Between loads: V changes by −w·Δx. At each concentrated force: V jumps ±P.
4V = 0 at right end — If not, recheck reactions.
5M from V — M starts at 0 (unless fixed support). ΔM = area under V diagram between two points.
6Shape rules — V constant → M linear. V linear → M parabolic. V = 0 → M is max/min.
7At concentrated couple — M jumps by the couple value. V is unaffected.
Check: M must be 0 at both ends for simply-supported beams. If M ≠ 0 at a free end, you have an error.
Free Body Diagram — Master Procedure
1Isolate — Draw the body SEPARATED from all supports and connections. Sketch its shape.
2Weight — Draw weight W = mg at the center of gravity (↓ always).
3Applied forces — Draw all given external forces and couples at their points of application.
4Reactions — At each support, replace it with the correct reaction forces/moments (see support table). Label each unknown with a letter (Ax, Ay, By, etc.).
5Dimensions — Add all relevant distances needed for moment calculations.
6Assume directions — If unknown, assume positive directions. If your answer is negative, the actual direction is opposite.
FBD Checklist (check EVERY time):
  • ☐ Body is completely isolated (no supports touching it)
  • ☐ All external forces shown
  • ☐ All support reactions shown with correct type and direction
  • ☐ Weight included (unless negligible/not given)
  • ☐ All dimensions/angles labeled
  • ☐ Coordinate axes shown
Problem-Solving Strategy

The 5-Step Method

1READ — Identify what's given, what's asked. Sketch the setup.
2FBD — Draw a complete free body diagram. Count unknowns. Confirm solvable (unknowns ≤ equations).
3EQUATIONS — Write equilibrium equations. Choose moment points wisely to minimize simultaneous equations.
4SOLVE — Solve equations. Keep units. Check signs.
5CHECK — Verify with an unused equation. Do directions make physical sense?

Moment Point Selection (Key Skill)

  • Take moments about a point where two unknowns intersect → one equation, one unknown
  • For horizontal beam: take ΣM at left pin → solve for right roller reaction directly
  • Sum forces in direction ⊥ to multiple unknowns → eliminates them

Distributed Loads

Uniform load w (force/length): Resultant: R = w × L (at midpoint of loaded region) Triangular load (0 to w_max): Resultant: R = ½ × w_max × L (at ⅓ from heavy end)
Common Traps & Mistakes
Trap 1: Wrong angle reference. θ measured from x-axis: Fx = F cos θ, Fy = F sin θ. θ measured from y-axis: Fx = F sin θ, Fy = F cos θ. ALWAYS check which axis θ is measured from.
Trap 2: Missing reaction at fixed support. A fixed (built-in) support has 3 reactions: Ax, Ay, AND MA (moment). Don't forget the moment reaction.
Trap 3: Wrong moment arm. The moment arm d is the PERPENDICULAR distance from the point to the LINE OF ACTION, not the distance to the point of application.
Trap 4: Sign errors in moments. Pick a sign convention (+ = CCW) and stick with it. Every force's moment must be evaluated: does it cause CW or CCW rotation about the chosen point?
Trap 5: Distributed load location. The resultant of a uniform load acts at the CENTER. The resultant of a triangular load acts at ⅓ from the HEAVY end. Don't confuse these.
Trap 6: Assuming cable can push. Cables, ropes, and strings can only be in TENSION. If your calculation gives negative tension → cable is slack → force = 0.
Trap 7: Not simplifying 3D moments. For 3D cross products, use the determinant method carefully. Sign errors in cross products are the #1 source of wrong answers.
Trap 8: Internal vs external forces. Newton's 3rd law pairs CANCEL in FBDs. Only include forces from OUTSIDE the isolated system. If analyzing the whole beam, internal hinge forces don't appear.
